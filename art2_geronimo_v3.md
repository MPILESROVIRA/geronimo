**Abstract**

Although still controversial, epigenetic inheritance has been reported
to play an important role in adaptation to new environmental
constraints. In this context, this study aims to provide new evidence
for or against this hypothesis by investigating, through a dedicated
experimental design in Japanese quails (*Coturnix japonica*), the
transmission of DNA methylation, its role as a vector for transmitting
environmental effects across generations and the implication for
selection. The design included two lines (control and genistein-exposed)
followed over four generations (G0--G3). The quails were phenotyped for
body weight, slaughter and behavioral traits. The DNA methylation
profiles of these animals were assessed using Reduced Representation
Bisulfite Sequencing and used to build an epigenetic similarity matrix
among individuals. First, we explored the transmissibility of DNA
methylation and its potential role as a vector for the transmission of
genistein effects across generations using transmissibility models.
Next, we investigated the relative contribution of epigenetic to
phenotypic variability for each trait using mixed models that included
both genetic and epigenetic random effects. Finally, we evaluated the
benefit of including epigenetic information into evaluation model by
comparing the predictive ability of a classical BLUP model with derived
GOBLUP models for predicting transmissible potential.

Our results indicated that the transmissibility of methylation profiles
was moderate, ranging from 0.26 to 0.27. We found no evidence to support
the hypothesis that DNA methylation is the vector of transmission of
genistein effect across generations. Additionally, since sire and dam
path coefficients estimated in the transmissibility models did not
significantly deviate from 0.5, we could not reject the hypothesis that
the inheritance of methylation profiles is of purely genetic origin. In
terms of phenotypic variance, DNA methylation accounted for
approximately 40% of the phenotypic variability in body weight-related
traits and contributed moderately (15%) to one of the behavioural traits
assessed. Despite this contribution, the inclusion of epigenetic
information in derived GOBLUP models did not significantly improve the
predictive ability for transmissible potential compared to the classical
BLUP model for none of the trait.

In conclusion, while DNA methylation significantly contributes to
phenotypic variability, its integration into current selection models
does not provide a clear or consistent advantage. Further investigation
into additional epigenetic marks, such as histone modifications or
microRNA expression, may be necessary to fully elucidate the role of
epigenetics in adaptation to environmental constraints.

**Keywords:** DNA methylation, Transmissibility, Japanese quail,
Epigenetics, Animal selection, Genistein.

**Introduction**

Evolutionary studies have reported that non-genetic information that
drives evolution and natural selection can be inherited across
generations. Unlike DNA, non-genetic information is susceptible to
environmental modifications, making it a key factor in explaining how
wild species adapt to environmental constraints (Bonduriansky et al.,
2012; Jablonka and Lamb, 2008; O'Dea et al., 2016). Among the factors
contributing to non-genetic inheritance (Danchin et al., 2011; Mameli,
2004), epigenetic marks such as DNA methylation, non-coding RNAs and
histone modification, have received considerable attention. Numerous
studies have highlighted the role of epigenetics in shaping traits in
animal and plant (Charlesworth et al., 2017; Quadrana and Colot, 2016;
Thompson et al., 2020) with several studies reporting that epigenetic
marks are transgenerationally transmitted (Miska and Ferguson-Smith,
2016; Sabarís et al., 2023; van Otterdijk and Michels, 2016). However,
the concept of transgenerational epigenetic inheritance remains a
subject of debate (Bird, 2024; Loison, 2018; Vogt, 2022) particularly in
animal species, where empirical demonstration of its occurrence is
challenging (van Otterdijk and Michels, 2016). Indeed, environmental
changes experienced by a pregnant female can directly influence both her
developing offspring and the primordial germ cells carried by the embryo
(Perez and Lehner, 2019). Consequently, transgenerational epigenetic
transmission is only observable in the third generation (David et al.,
2019). Studies aiming to demonstrate transgenerational environmental
effect often rely on phenotypic comparisons between groups of animals
whose ancestors experienced different environmental conditions
(Braunschweig et al., 2012; Brun et al., 2015; Leroux et al., 2017). The
main drawback of such comparisons is that the phenotypic difference
observed between the two groups may be the result of genetic drift, even
if special attention has been devoted to limiting these differences by
using a mirrored design, as in Leroux et al. (2017). To address this
issue, David and Ricard developed the transmissibility model (2019) and
the transmissibility model with environment (2023) which allow testing
whether the transmission of information from parents to offspring
deviates from purely genetic inheritance (i.e path coefficients of
transmission different from 0.5) and whether additional covariance
exists between animals sharing the same environment that is transmitted
across generations. Comparison of these two models using a likelihood
ratio test (LRT) to detect vertical transmission of environmental
effects has proven to be more conservative and powerful than the
classical test of phenotypic difference between groups. Using a
multi-generational experimental design in Japanese quails (*Coturnix
japonica*), this study aims to provide new insights into the role of
epigenetics in the construction and transmission of traits.
Specifically, we seek to: 1) evaluate the transmissibility of DNA
methylation across generations and its role as a vector for vertical
transmission of environmental effects using transmissibility models, 2)
estimate the relative contributions of genetic and epigenetic factors to
the variability of traits and 3) assess the practical benefits of
incorporating epigenetic information into animal selection models.

**Material and methods**

**Experimental design and phenotypes**

An experimental study was carried out using Japanese quails at the INRAE
UE 1295 PEAT Poultry Experimental Facility (Nouzilly, France; DOI:
10.15454/1.5572326250887292E12). The founding population comprised 23
males and 23 females from distinct families, which were mated to produce
an average of nine cross-family offspring per pair, of which three (two
females and one male) were retained to serve as parents for the
subsequent generation. Each selected male offspring from a given pair
was then mated with two sisters from a different pair: one sister
received a standard diet, while the other was administered a diet
supplemented with genistein for 30 days. Genistein, a plant derived
phytoestrogen, is hypothesized to influence phenotypic traits through
epigenetic mechanisms, particularly alterations in DNA methylation
patterns (Silva et al., 2019). This approach established two distinct
lines: a control line (Geni--, derived from females without genistein
exposure) and a treatment line (Geni+, derived from females exposed to
genistein).

Following the establishment of these lines, three successive generations
were produced for each line using a mirrored single-pair mating strategy
at each generation. The total population size across all generations was
1,566 individuals (Fig 1). Phenotyping of animals from G0 to G3 was the
following: at approximately 16 weeks of age, quails underwent a
behavioural assessment in a square arena (80 cm length × 80 cm width ×
80 cm height) constructed from white wood with a beige linoleum floor.
Each individual was observed for 300 seconds, during which the time
spent in designated zones (a central 20 cm × 20 cm area or the
periphery) and locomotor activity---measured as the total distance
travelled---were recorded. Body weights were measured at one, four, and
seven weeks of age. At slaughter (around 30 weeks of age), body and
abdominal adipose tissue weights were measured.

**Methylation data**

A blood sample was collected from each animal (founders included) at 7
weeks of age to obtain DNA methylation profile using Reduced
Representation Bisulfite Sequencing (RRBS) (Gu et al., 2010). In total
1343 blood samples were analyzed, distributed across 14 plates
randomized by families, line and generation. The BISCUIT pipeline (Zhou
et al., 2024) with default parameters was used for analyzing methylation
bisulfite sequencing data and also for detecting single-nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs) by using the *Coturnix japonica* 2.0 genome
assembly. From the initial analyzed blood samples, 71 were removed
because of a too few number of CpG sites (\<120,000) and 5 were removed
because of duplicated animals. Subsequently, a series of filters were
applied to the initial 1,606,750 CpG sites: First, sites with more than
20% missing values were excluded. Next, sites corresponding to SNPs were
removed. Sites exhibiting low variability in methylation percentage
(standard deviation $\leq 0.05$) were then excluded, reducing the count
to 111,168 sites. Finally, sites located on sex chromosomes were
removed, yielding a final set of 108,390 CpG sites for 1266 animals. Per
sample, for each CpG sites, methylation ratio (*p*) was calculated as
number of reads with « C »/(number of reads with « C » + number of reads
with « T ». To correct methylation ratio for less informative CpG sites,
empirical bayes beta-binomial (Brandel, 2004) was applied for CpG sites
covered by less than 10 mapped reads :
$p_{new,ij} = p_{\text{ij}}*c_{\text{ij}} + \frac{\alpha_{j}}{c_{\text{ij}} + \alpha_{j} + \beta_{j}}$
where $p_{\text{ij}}$, $p_{new,ij}$ and $c_{\text{ij}}$ are the initial,
corrected methylation ratio and coverage of CpG site $j$ for animal $i$,
respectively; $\alpha_{j}$ and $\beta_{j}$ are the empirical Bayes Beta
prior parameters for CpG site $j$, estimated using the method of moments
from site-wise methylation means and variances corrected for binomial
sampling noise. The methylation ratio for the CpG site of sample with no
information at all ($c_{\text{ij}} = 0$, representing less than 0.2% of
the data) were replaced by the average methylation ratio of CpG site
$j$. In addition, the methylation information of 1 animal (female in G2
Geni- line) was excluded because of outlier value. Thus, the final
methylation data set consisted in methylation ratio of 1265 animals
(1221 phenotyped) for 108,390 CpG sites. After correction by the plate
effect, corrected methylation data was centred by column leading to
matrices $\mathbf{E}_{\text{all}}$ and $\mathbf{E}_{\text{phen}}$ for
all and phenotyped animals, respectively. Methylation similarity
matrices between individuals were then constructed using a linear kernel
:
$\mathbf{\Gamma}_{\text{all}} = 1265\frac{\mathbf{E}_{\text{all}}\mathbf{E'}_{\text{all}}}{\text{tr}\left( \mathbf{E}_{\text{all}}\mathbf{E'}_{\text{all}} \right)}$
and
$\mathbf{\Gamma}_{\text{phen}} = 1221\frac{\mathbf{E}_{\text{phen}}\mathbf{E'}_{\text{phen}}}{\text{tr}\left( \mathbf{E}_{\text{phen}}\mathbf{E'}_{\text{phen}} \right)}$
for all and phenotyped animals, respectively.

**Methods**

This study aimed to evaluate in quails 1) the transmissibility of DNA
methylation across generations and its potential role as a vector for
transmitting environmental effects, 2) the relative contributions of
genetic and epigenetic factors to phenotypic variability, and 3) the
benefits of incorporating epigenetic information into animal selection.

***Transmissibility of methylation across generations***

The transmissibility of methylation was assessed by applying the method
proposed by David et al. (2022) which consists in applying the
transmissibility models (David and Ricard, 2023, 2019) to a realization
of pseudo phenotypes $\mathbf{y}\ $generated by
$\mathbf{y}\sim N(\mathbf{0},\mathbf{\Gamma}_{\text{all}})$. The
transmissibility model is for animal $i$:
$y_{i} = \mathbf{x}_{\mathbf{i}}\mathbf{\beta} + t_{i} + e_{i}$, where
$y_{i}$ is the pseudo-phenotype of individual $i$, $\mathbf{\beta}$ is
the vector of fixed effects with known incidence matrix
$\mathbf{x}_{\mathbf{i}}$, $e_{i}$ is the residual with
$\mathbf{e}\sim N\left( 0,\mathbf{I}\sigma_{e}^{2} \right)$ where
$\mathbf{I}$ is the identity matrix, $t_{i}$ is the transmissible
potential of animal $i$ with
$\mathbf{t}\sim N\left( \mathbf{0},\mathbf{M}\sigma_{t}^{2} \right)$,
where $\mathbf{M}$ is the matrix of transmission between individuals
(transmission relationship matrix),
$\mathbf{M}^{- 1} = \mathbf{L}^{'}\mathbf{D}^{- 1}\mathbf{L}$, where
$\mathbf{L}$ is a lower triangular matrix with 1s on the diagonal and
the negatives of the sire and dam's coefficients of transmission
($\omega_{s},\omega_{d}$, respectively) as off-diagonal entries that
have to be estimated. In the transmissibility model, $\mathbf{D}$ is a
diagonal matrix with variances of the transmissibility sampling relative
to $\sigma_{t}^{2}$ as components ($\delta_{i}$), i.e., considering no
inbreeding:
$\delta_{i} = \left( 1 - \omega_{s}^{2} - \omega_{d}^{2} \right)$ if
both parents of animal $i$ are known,
$\delta_{i} = \left( 1 - \omega_{d}^{2} \right)$ for animals of an
unknown sire, $\delta_{i} = \left( 1 - \omega_{s}^{2} \right)$ for
animals of an unknown dam, and 1 for animals for which both parents are
unknown. If $\omega_{s} = \omega_{d} = 0.5$ then the $\mathbf{M}$ matrix
is the **A** matrix. In the transmissibility model with environment, the
$\mathbf{D}$ matrix includes an additional positive, and upper bounded
by $\left( 1 - \omega_{d}^{2} - \omega_{s}^{2} \right)$, covariance $r$
between animals sharing the same environmental effect due to genistein
(i.e. embryos of females eating genistein (Karami et al., 2025)) that
has to be estimated. Fixed effects included in the models were the sex
(2 levels), generation (5 levels) and the direct effect of the genistein
(3 levels). Genistein may have a direct multigenerational impact by
modifying the methylation pattern of the animal eating it (females G0
Geni+ line), its embryo and its primordial germ cells. However, since
blood samples were collected prior to genistein ingestion, the direct
effect of genistein on the methylation profile of animal eating it
cannot yet be observable. Variance components, path coefficients and
covariance $r$ of the transmissibility models are estimated with ASReml
(Gilmour et al., 2015) and the freely available OWN Fortran program
developed by David (2023). Pseudo phenotypes simulation and estimation
are repeated 238 times in order to obtain the distribution of the
variance component, path coefficient and $r$ estimates. Using the
parameter estimates in the transmissibility models, we computed the
proportion of transmitted variance as
$\frac{\sigma_{t}^{2}}{\sigma_{t}^{2} + \sigma_{e}^{2}}$ , the sire and
dam transmissibilities as
$\omega_{s}\frac{\sigma_{t}^{2}}{\sigma_{t}^{2} + \sigma_{e}^{2}},\omega_{d}\frac{\sigma_{t}^{2}}{\sigma_{t}^{2} + \sigma_{e}^{2}}$,
respectively and the correlation $\rho$ between animals with known
parents sharing the same environment as
$\rho = \frac{r}{1 - \omega_{d}^{2} - \omega_{s}^{2}}$. In addition to
the transmissibility models, a classical animal genetic model was
applied to the pseudo phenotypes in order to obtain the heritability
$h_{\text{epi}}^{2}$ of methylation profile.

***Relative importance of genetic and epigenetic***

We evaluated the relative importance of genetic and epigenetic in the
variability of 7 traits: body weight at 1 week of age
($\text{BW}_{1w}$), average daily gain between 1 and 4 weeks
($\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$), between 4 and 7 weeks $\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$ and
between 7 weeks and slaughter ($\text{ADG}_{7 - sl}$), the abdominal
tissue weight relative to body weight (AdipWeightperBW), the time spent
in the centre of the area (Centre Duration) and the total distance
(TotalDist) covered by the animal during the behavioral test. To
transforms non-normal data into normal distribution, the Box-Cox
transformation was used for AdipWeightperBW, square-root transformation
was used for Centre Duration and Tukey transformation for TotalDist. The
general form of the model (G+EPI model) applied to each phenotype was:
$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\text{Xβ}} + \mathbf{\text{Zd}} + \mathbf{Wm + Vepi} + \mathbf{e}$
where $\mathbf{y}$ is the vector of phenotype under consideration,
$\mathbf{\beta}$ is the vector of fixed effects, **d** and $\mathbf{m}$
are the vectors of direct and maternal additive genetic effects
$\begin{bmatrix}
\mathbf{d} \\
\mathbf{m} \\
\end{bmatrix}\sim N\left( 0,\begin{bmatrix}
\sigma_{d}^{2} & \sigma_{\text{dm}} \\
\sigma_{\text{dm}} & \sigma_{m}^{2} \\
\end{bmatrix}\mathbf{\bigotimes A} \right)$, $\mathbf{\text{epi}}$ is
the vector of epigenetic effect
$\mathbf{\text{epi}}\sim N\left( 0,\mathbf{\Gamma}_{\text{phen}}\sigma_{\text{epi}}^{2} \right)$
and $\mathbf{e}$ the residuals
$\mathbf{e}\sim N\left( 0,\mathbf{I}\sigma_{e}^{2} \right)$,
$\mathbf{X,Z,W}$ and $\mathbf{V}$ are known incidence matrices. Maternal
effects were included for body weight and ADG traits only (i.e; body
weight-related traits). Fixed effects included in the model were the
sex, age when the trait was recorded, generation, direct genistein
effect and rearing condition (cage of same or different sex) for traits
recorded after week 20. The same model excluding epigenetic factor (G
model) was also applied to each phenotype in order to test, using LRT,
the significance of epigenetic variance. Since the test corresponds to a
test at the boundary of the parameter space, the asymptotic distribution
of the LRT under the null hypothesis is a 50:50 mixture, $\chi_{0}^{2}$
and $\chi_{1}^{2}$ (Stram and Lee, 1994). The direct, maternal and total
heritabilities were computed as
$\ h_{d}^{2} = \frac{\sigma_{d}^{2}}{\sigma_{T}^{2}}$,
$h_{m}^{2} = \frac{\sigma_{m}^{2}}{\sigma_{T}^{2}}$,
$h_{T}^{2} = \frac{\sigma_{d}^{2} + {2\sigma}_{\text{dm}} + \sigma_{m}^{2}}{\sigma_{T}^{2}}$
(Eaglen and Bijma, 2009) and the relative importance of epigenetic to
the phenotypic variance as
$c_{\text{epi}}^{2} = \frac{\sigma_{\text{epi}}^{2}}{\sigma_{T}^{2}}$
with
$\sigma_{T}^{2} = \sigma_{d}^{2} + \sigma_{m}^{2} + \sigma_{\text{dm}} + \sigma_{\text{epi}}^{2} + \sigma_{e}^{2}$,
with several components fixed to 0 depending on the model applied (with
or without maternal effect, with or without epigenetic effects). To
avoid any confusion between the heritabilities of the trait obtained in
the 2 models, the heritabilities not mediated by epigenetic (G+EPI
model) were labelled hereafter
$(h_{d,nm}^{2},h_{m,nm}^{2},h_{T,nm}^{2})$ and the heritabilities
estimated using the G model were named "full" heritabilities.

**Benefit of including epigenetic information for animal selection**

The benefit of including epigenetic information for selection was
assessed by comparing the predictive ability of a classical BLUP model
to different derived GOBLUP models (Christensen et al., 2021) described
below. For that purpose, we considered that selection of future
reproducers was performed just before reproduction starts (i.e. 20 weeks
of age) based on phenotypes already recorded in candidate. Phenotypes
from the 518 animals in G3 were removed and the different models to
compare were applied to the remaining data (G0 to G2 phenotypes). Then
the correlation between the transmissibility potential predicted with
the different model and the phenotype of animals in G3 corrected for
fixed effects were calculated as the predictive ability to correctly
predict the transmissible potential of animals in G2. The different
models to compare were:

-   the classical BLUP:
    $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\text{Xβ}} + \mathbf{\text{Zd}} + \mathbf{\text{Wm}} + \mathbf{e}$,
    where notations are the same as in previous presented model. The
    transmissibility potential predicted with this model is purely
    genetic: ${\widehat{d}}_{i} + {\widehat{m}}_{k}$ for animal $i$ of
    dam $k$.

-   The AOBLUP model (a classical GOBLUP considering genetic instead of
    genomic relationship matrix) applied in 2 steps: model 1:
    $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\text{Xβ}} + \mathbf{\text{Zd}} + \mathbf{Wm + Vepi} + \mathbf{e}$
    then model 2:
    $\widehat{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} + \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{u}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}}$**,**
    where $\widehat{\mathbf{\text{epi}}}$ are the prediction of
    epigenetic effects obtained with model 1. Genetic parameters were
    considered as known in both models (estimated per trait on the full
    dataset in the previous step for model 1 and estimated on pseudo
    methylation phenotype in the first step of analysis:
    $h_{\text{epi}}^{2} = 0.18$), the predicted transmissibility
    potential is for animal $i$ of dam $k$:
    ${\widehat{d}}_{i} + {\widehat{m}}_{k} + {\widehat{u}}_{epi,i}$

-   The TOBLUP model (an AOBLUP model considering a transmissibility
    model for the epigenetic effect) applied in 2 steps: model 1:
    $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{\text{Xβ}} + \mathbf{\text{Zd}} + \mathbf{Wm + Vepi} + \mathbf{e}$
    then model 2:
    $\widehat{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{\beta}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} + \mathbf{Z}\mathbf{t}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}} + \mathbf{e}_{\mathbf{\text{epi}}}$.
    The second model corresponded to a transmissibility model or a
    transmissibility model with environment (T~E~OBLUP). In this second
    model the transmissibility and coefficients of the transmissibility
    matrix were considered as known and fixed to the values obtained in
    the *Transmissibility of methylation across generations* section
    thus the same for all traits. The predicted transmissibility
    potential is for animal $i$ of dam $k$:
    ${\widehat{d}}_{i} + {\widehat{m}}_{k} + {\widehat{t}}_{i}$

Fixed effects included in models 2 were the sex, generation, direct
genistein effect. Standard error of each predictive ability
($\text{pa}$) was approximated by
$\frac{1 - \text{pa}^{2}}{\sqrt{518 - 3}}$ (Gnambs, 2023). This
corresponds to the lower bound of the standard error of predictive
ability, i.e. assuming perfect prediction of transmissibility
potential.**\

**Results**

***Transmissibility of methylation across generations***

Variance components, path coefficients and $r$ value obtained with the
transmissibility model and the transmissibility model with environment
applied to methylation related pseudo phenotypes are presented in Table
1. Of the 238 replicates, 69 were excluded due to either a negative LRT
value when comparing the two transmissibility models (n=63) or a $\rho$
value exceeding 1 (n=6). Estimates of variance components and path
coefficient were similar across both models. The transmissibility of the
methylation profile was moderate (0.26±0.07 and 0.27±0.087 in the
transmissibility model and the transmissibility model with environment,
respectively). While the dam path coefficient estimate was on average
larger than the sire one (0.48 versus 0.43 or 0.42 depending on the
model), the two coefficients did not differ significantly from each
other and were not significantly different from 0.5. The correlation
between transmissibility sampling of animals exposed to genistein was
moderate ($\rho$=0.27), but not significantly different from 0. The
distribution of this estimate deviated substantially from normality (see
Appendix 1), exhibiting a point mass at 0 and a broad range of values.

***Relative importance of genetic and epigenetic***

Variance components estimated for each trait under an animal genetic
model---with or without an epigenetic random effect---are presented
graphically in Figure 2 and in Appendix 2. Likelihood ratio tests
revealed no significant epigenetic contribution to phenotypic variance
for adipWeightPerBW or totalDist. For most body weight-related traits,
epigenetics accounted for approximately 40% of the phenotypic variance:
$c_{\text{epi}}^{2}$ was estimated at 0.43±0.08, 0.45±0.10 and 0.38±0.08
for $\text{BW}_{1w}$, $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$ and $\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$,
respectively. In contrast, the epigenetic contribution to
$\text{ADG}_{7 - sl}$ was low ($c_{\text{epi}}^{2} =$0.08±0.09) and not
significantly different from zero. The LRT comparing the 2 nested models
(genetic model with or without epigenetic effects) yielded a value lower
than 1.92 (0.74). The relative epigenetic contribution to phenotypic
variance for Centre Duration was moderate:
$c_{\text{epi}}^{2} =$0.15±0.08. When significant, the inclusion of
epigenetic information primarily captured residual variance for body
weight-related traits, reducing it by 66%, 86% and 72% for
$\text{BW}_{1w}$, $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$ and $\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$,
respectively. The direct genetic variance was also reduced, but to a
lesser extent (39%, 20% and 27% for the same traits, respectively).
Maternal genetic variance was generally unaffected by the inclusion of
epigenetic information, except for $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$, where it
decreased by 36%; however, the relative importance of maternal genetic
effects remained low (\<8%). The total heritability not mediated by
epigenetic $h_{T,nm}^{2}$ remained large for these traits: 0.49, 0.48
and 0.58 for $\text{BW}_{1w}$, $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$ and
$\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$, respectively. For the behavioural trait centre
duration, the pattern was reversed compared to body weight-related
traits: the inclusion of epigenetic effects led to an 64% reduction in
genetic variance which was of very low magnitude ($h_{d}^{2}$=0.05±0.03)
while residual variance was only reduced by 13%. The heritability not
mediated by epigenetic is therefore extremely low $h_{d,nm}^{2}$=0.02.

**Benefit of including epigenetic information for animal selection**

Evaluation of the benefit of including epigenetic information for
selection was restricted to traits recorded before 20 week of age and
for which a significant epigenetic variance was detected in the previous
step of analysis ($\text{BW}_{1w}$, $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$ and
$\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$ and Centre Duration). The predictive ability of the
different models for each trait is presented in Fig 3. For none of the
trait did the inclusion of epigenetic information significantly improve
the prediction of transmissibility potential. For body weight-related
traits, the predictive ability of the BLUP model was moderate, with
values ranging from 0.38 to 0.46. The predictive ability of the derived
GOBLUP models showed only slight, non-significant improvements for
$\text{BW}_{1w}$, (average increases of 1.5%) and a marginal decrease
for $\text{ADG}_{1 - 4}$ and $\text{ADG}_{4 - 7}$ (around 2% on
average). No differences in predictive ability were observed among the
different derived GOBLUP models. For the behavioral trait Centre
Duration, the predictive ability of the BLUP model was almost null
\<0.01. The inclusion of epigenetic information led to a non-significant
improvement in predictive ability that remained low, ranging from 0.037
to 0.042 depending on the derived GOBLUP model.

**Discussion**

Following recent findings from the same quail experiment suggesting
vertical transmission of genistein effect across generations (Karami et
al., 2025), our study investigated if DNA methylation measured by
-cost-effective- RRBS was the vector of this transmission. Results
obtained did not support such hypothesis ($\rho$ not significantly
different from 0). Nevertheless, this does not imply that epigenetic
modifications are not responsible for this transmission. Indeed, RRBS
only provides a partial view of CpG sites (\~18%), primarily focused on
CpG islands (Beck et al., 2022), and DNA methylation represents just one
aspect of the epigenetic changes induced by genistein. Genistein also
triggers histone modifications and alterations in miRNA expression
(Silva et al., 2019), which warrant further investigation. The
mechanisms driving transgenerational epigenetic inheritance remain
controversial (Bird, 2024; Vogt, 2022). By applying the transmissibility
model to pseudo methylation phenotypes, even if the dam path coefficient
of transmission tended to be larger than the sire one, we cannot reject
the hypothesis of a purely genetic inheritance of DNA methylation (both
path coefficients did not differ from to 0.5). The moderate heritability
observed for DNA methylation in our study aligns with the one reported
by McRae et al (2014).

We analyzed the effects of DNA methylation on phenotypes using a mixed
model that considers epigenetics as an additional random effect
alongside genetic effects. This approach aligns with the conventional
integration of epigenetic within the mixed model framework (Malinowska
et al., 2022; Paiva et al., 2018; Varona et al., 2015). Nevertheless,
given the established role of DNA methylation in repressing mRNA, an
alternative model have recently been proposed that considers
---currently limited to single-locus cases---to explicitly capture the
regulatory mechanism (Ayres et al., 2025). However, expanding these
models to multiple loci and methylation sites introduces significant
complexities leading to challenges in estimation and identifiability. We
used a linear kernel to build the epigenetic similarity matrix between
individuals. The underlying assumption of this kernel is that the
effects of CpG sites on the phenotype are purely linear and additive.
Alternative kernels could have been employed, such as the polynomial
kernel, which assumes interaction effects between CpG sites, the
arc-cosine kernel, which accounts for nonlinear effects of individual
sites, or the Gaussian kernel, which combines nonlinearity with
interactions between sites (Montesinos-López et al., 2021). When applied
with microbiota data, comparisons of these different kernels have
however revealed slight variability in the percentage of variance
explained (He et al., 2022). In our model genetic and epigenetic effects
were considered as independent, an assumption that may be questionable
given that epigenetic variation can be under genetic control. Zhou et
al. (2020) proposed the CORE GREML method to estimate variance
components for correlated random effects. However, our attempts to apply
this model to our dataset were unsuccessful. The relative contribution
of epigenetic to phenotypic variance that we estimated is
trait-specific. Notably, it remains relatively substantial for
weight-related traits, which aligns with findings reported by Kim et al.
(2009) and Rockwood et al.(2019) on the effects of genistein in mice,
mediated through modifications in methylation patterns (Silva et al.,
2019).

Our analysis did not reveal a clear or consistent across traits
advantage of integrating epigenetic information to predict transmissible
potential. Legarra and Christensen (2023) provided insights to
anticipate the benefits of incorporating omics data into the prediction
of breeding values, depending on the relative contribution of omics to
phenotypic variability and the full or omics-specific heritability.
Their findings suggest that omics data could be particularly
advantageous for traits with low full heritability, especially when the
proportion of phenotypic variance explained by omics is small
($c_{\text{epi}}^{2}$) but the omics component itself is highly
heritable ($h_{\text{epi}}^{2}$). However, these conclusions should be
interpreted with caution, as they were derived under the assumption of
perfect prediction of omic effects, whose accuracy - as pointed out by
the authors - depends on $c_{\text{epi}}^{2}$. In our case, the full
heritability of body weight related traits were high, it is therefore
not surprising -- given the remarks made by Legarra and Christensen
(2023) - to not highlighted improvement in predictive ability when
including epigenetic information. The full heritability of Centre
Duration was low and therefore a good candidate for an improvement in
predictive ability when epigenetic information included. Indeed, an
improvement in predictive ability is observed when epigenetic
information is included. However, the extremely low values and lack of
precision of these estimates preclude any definitive conclusions. It
should be noted that in our study, we have considered the same genetic
(or transmissibility) parameters for all traits when analysing
$\widehat{\mathbf{\text{epi}}}$ in models 2, these parameters being
obtained from the epigenetic similarity matrix using pseudo phenotype.
This implicitly hypothesis that CpG sites implicated in the phenotypic
variance of the different traits are the same or numerous and/or
randomly sampled. Numerous studies have explored the benefits of
including omics data (transcriptomic, metabolomic, epigenetic,
microbiota\...) to enhance the accuracy of phenotype prediction
(Graverand et al., 2025; Haas et al., 2025; Jové-Juncà et al., 2026;
Maltecca et al., 2025). However, far fewer (Croiseau et al., 2025; Guo
et al., 2025) have focused on the potential advantage of omics data for
predicting transmissible potential (BV if purely genetic), despite its
central importance in selection and the need for it to remain a focal
point of research.

**Conclusion**

The results of this study confirm that DNA methylation significantly
contributes to the phenotypic variability of several traits. However, we
did not identify a mode of transmission for these epigenetic marks that
is independent of genetic influence, which would allow them to serve as
a vector for transmitting environmental effects across generations.
Furthermore, the integration of DNA methylation data into current
selection models did not yield a clear or consistent advantage. Thus,
further research into additional epigenetic marks, such as histone
modifications or microRNA expression, may be necessary to fully
elucidate the elucidate the role of epigenetics in adaptation to
environmental constraints.
